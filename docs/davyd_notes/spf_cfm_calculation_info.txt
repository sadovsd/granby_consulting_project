Why no pedestrian volume input?
The HSM has two different pedestrian models depending on intersection type. For signalized intersections, 
they had enough data to build a model that uses actual pedestrian crossing counts (PedVol) as a direct 
input. For stop-controlled intersections like yours, they didn't have enough ped count data to do that. 
So instead they built a simpler model that uses proxy variables — things that indicate pedestrians are likely present.

Number of approaches with left-turn lanes (signalized): At a signalized intersection, some approaches might 
have a dedicated left-turn lane with its own arrow signal. This counts how many of the 3 or 4 approaches have 
that. Yours is stop-controlled, not signalized, so this doesn't apply. The unsignalized version (which you 
already set to 1) is the one that matters for you.

Number of approaches with right-turn lanes (signalized): Same idea — some signalized intersections have 
dedicated right-turn-only lanes. Think of a busy intersection where there's a separate lane just for 
right turns with its own island. Your intersection doesn't have a signal at all, so this is irrelevant.

Number of approaches with left-turn signal phasing: This is asking how many of the approaches have a 
protected left-turn phase — meaning a green arrow where only left-turners go. Again, no signal at 
your intersection.

Type of left-turn signal phasing for Legs 1–4: This gets more specific — is each leg's left turn 
"permissive" (you yield to oncoming traffic), "protected" (green arrow), or "protected-permissive" 
(arrow then yield)? No signal, so Not Applicable.

Right-turn-on-red prohibited: At some signalized intersections, right-on-red is banned. Not relevant to you.

Red light cameras: Does the signal have cameras that ticket red-light runners? No signal, no cameras.

PedVol: Total daily pedestrian crossings at a signalized intersection. The signalized pedestrian SPF 
uses this as a direct input variable. Your stop-controlled model uses schools/alcohol instead.

A pedestrian crossing with curb ramps and extensions has a CMF of 0.63 Cmfclearinghouse, meaning a 
37% reduction in crashes. However, that CMF has a "Cannot Be Rated" star quality, meaning the study 
behind it wasn't super rigorous. It's from a 2004 ITE report. https://cmfclearinghouse.fhwa.dot.gov/detail.php?facid=1786
